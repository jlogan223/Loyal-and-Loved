Introduction

If you have ever picked up a repeat flea treatment from your vet and winced at the bill, you are not alone. UK pet owners routinely pay 30-70% more for medicines bought over the vet's counter than the same products sold through licensed online pharmacies. A three-month supply of Bravecto that costs £45 at the surgery might be £25 online, and the saving only grows with long-term medications for conditions like arthritis or hypothyroidism.

The good news: buying pet prescriptions online is completely legal in the UK, provided the pharmacy is registered with the Veterinary Medicines Directorate (VMD) and you have a valid prescription. This guide explains the regulations, compares the main online pharmacies, and shows you how to make the switch without any risk to your pet's care.

Quick Answer

Yes, it is legal to buy pet medicines online in the UK. You need a written prescription from your vet for POM-V medicines (like Bravecto, NexGard, Advocate). Many flea collars, basic wormers, and supplements are available without any prescription. Online pharmacies are typically 30-70% cheaper than vet dispensaries. Always check the pharmacy appears on the VMD's register of online retailers.

Is It Legal? UK Veterinary Medicines Regulations

The Veterinary Medicines Regulations 2013 (VMR) govern how animal medicines are classified, prescribed, and sold in the UK. Every veterinary medicine falls into one of four categories, and each category determines who can supply it and whether you need a prescription.

Category What it means Prescription needed? Examples
POM-V Prescription Only Medicine, Veterinarian. Only a vet can prescribe after a clinical assessment. Yes, from a vet Bravecto, NexGard, Advocate, Metacam, Onsior, Thyronorm
POM-VPS Prescription from a vet, pharmacist, or Suitably Qualified Person (SQP). No clinical assessment required. Yes, but wider range of prescribers Some prescription diets, certain flea/tick products
NFA-VPS Non-food animal. Must be supplied by a vet, pharmacist, or SQP, but no written prescription needed. No prescription, but must be supplied by a qualified person Frontline spot-on (some pack sizes), Drontal, Milbemax
AVM-GSL General Sales List. No restrictions on retail supply whatsoever. No Basic flea collars, some wormers, herbal supplements

The critical point: any online retailer selling POM-V, POM-VPS, or NFA-VPS medicines must be registered with the VMD and must display the phrase "registered internet retailer of veterinary medicines" on their website. If a site does not show this, do not buy from it.

The written prescription itself must include the prescriber's name and qualifications, the animal's identification, the medicine name and dosage, and any warnings. A prescription is valid for up to six months from the date it was signed, giving you time to shop around.

How Online Pet Pharmacies Work

The process is straightforward once you have your prescription in hand. Here is the typical flow:

  1. Get a prescription from your vet. At the end of a consultation, ask your vet for a written prescription. Under the RCVS Code of Professional Conduct, your vet must provide one if you ask. There is usually a small fee (see How to Ask Your Vet for a Prescription below).
  2. Choose your pharmacy. Pick a VMD-registered online pharmacy. All the pharmacies compared in this guide are registered.
  3. Place your order. Add the prescribed medication to your basket. Make sure the product name, strength, and quantity match your prescription exactly.
  4. Upload your prescription. Most pharmacies accept a photo or scan uploaded through their website. Some accept email. A few still require you to post the original.
  5. Verification and dispatch. A qualified pharmacist or vet checks the prescription against your order. Once verified, the medication is dispatched, usually within 1-2 working days.
  6. Delivery. Standard delivery takes 2-5 working days. Some pharmacies offer next-day delivery for an extra fee. Free delivery thresholds vary (typically £29-£49).

For non-prescription products (NFA-VPS and AVM-GSL), you simply add them to your basket and check out like any other online purchase. No upload step needed.

OTC vs Prescription: What You Can Buy Without a Vet

Not everything in your pet's medicine cabinet requires a trip to the vet. Understanding the difference can save you both time and money.

Products you CAN buy without a prescription

These fall under the NFA-VPS or AVM-GSL categories. You can order them from any VMD-registered retailer (or, for AVM-GSL, from any shop at all) without uploading a prescription:

  • Flea spot-on treatments such as Frontline (fipronil-based) and some Advantage products
  • Worming tablets including Drontal, Milbemax, and Panacur
  • Flea collars and basic repellent sprays
  • Supplements including joint support, dental chews, and probiotics
  • Ear and eye cleaners (non-medicated)

For OTC flea and worming products, you might also find competitive prices on Amazon, particularly for well-known brands like Frontline and Bob Martin. Amazon's 24-hour cookie means anything your reader buys within a day earns commission, not just the product they clicked on.

Products that DO require a prescription

These are POM-V medicines. Your vet must have examined your pet and written a prescription before any pharmacy can dispense them:

  • Isoxazoline flea/tick treatments: Bravecto, NexGard, NexGard Spectra, Simparica, Simparica Trio, Credelio
  • Prescription wormers: Milpro (milbemycin-based), Advocate (also covers fleas)
  • Pain relief: Metacam (meloxicam), Onsior (robenacoxib), Gabapentin, Tramadol
  • Chronic disease medications: Thyronorm (hypothyroidism), Vetmedin (heart disease), Zitac (gastric acid), Atopica (skin allergies)
  • Prescription diets: Royal Canin Veterinary, Hills Prescription Diet, Purina Pro Plan Veterinary Diets
  • Antibiotics and anti-fungals: all require a prescription

How to Ask Your Vet for a Prescription

This is the step that puts some owners off, but it should not. Under the RCVS Code of Professional Conduct, your vet is obliged to provide a written prescription if you request one. Here is how to handle it smoothly.

When to ask: The best time is at the end of a routine consultation or annual health check. If your pet is already on a repeat medication, you can phone the practice and request a prescription without booking a full consultation (though some practices require an annual review).

What it costs: Vets are allowed to charge a reasonable fee for writing a prescription. This is typically £10-£25, depending on the practice. Some practices charge per item, others charge a flat fee regardless of how many medications are listed. Even at the high end, the savings from buying online almost always exceed the prescription fee.

What you will receive: A written document (paper or sometimes electronic) that includes the vet's name and RCVS number, your name and address, the animal's name and species, the medicine name, strength, dosage instructions, and the date signed. The prescription is valid for up to six months.

What to say: Simply: "Could I have a written prescription for [medicine name] please? I'd like to purchase it from an online pharmacy." No justification needed. Some vets may mention their in-house dispensary prices, which is fair, but they cannot refuse the prescription.

Cost-Saving Tips

Switching to an online pharmacy is the biggest single saving, but there are other ways to reduce your pet medication bill.

Compare prices across pharmacies. Prices vary between online pharmacies, sometimes significantly. A quick check across three or four sites before ordering can save a few extra pounds per item. Bookmark the Quick Compare section above for easy reference.

Buy in bulk where safe to do so. Many flea and tick treatments come in 3-month or 6-month packs. The per-dose price is almost always lower in larger packs. Just check your prescription covers the quantity.

Look at free delivery thresholds. If you are close to a pharmacy's free delivery threshold (£29-£49), it may be worth adding a non-prescription item like food, treats, or supplements to avoid paying delivery charges.

Consider generic alternatives. Ask your vet whether a generic version of the prescribed medicine is available. Generic meloxicam (instead of branded Metacam) can be substantially cheaper with identical active ingredients.

Time your prescription requests. Since prescriptions last six months, ask for one at your pet's annual checkup. This covers two rounds of flea/tick treatment (spring and autumn) on a single prescription fee.

Check for OTC equivalents. If your vet prescribes a POM-V flea treatment, ask whether a non-prescription alternative would be suitable for your pet's circumstances. Not every pet needs the strongest product available, though for tick-heavy areas or multi-pet households, prescription-strength treatments are often worth it.

How to Check the VMD Register

Before buying from any online pet pharmacy, spend 30 seconds verifying they are legitimate. The Veterinary Medicines Directorate maintains a public register of all authorised online retailers.

  1. Go to the VMD Register of Online Retailers.
  2. Search by pharmacy name or browse the list.
  3. Confirm the business name matches the website you plan to buy from.

If a pharmacy is not on this register and is selling POM-V, POM-VPS, or NFA-VPS medicines, they are operating illegally. Do not buy from them. Counterfeit pet medicines are a real risk, and the RSPCA explicitly warns against sites that do not ask for a valid prescription before dispensing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to buy pet prescriptions online in the UK?

Yes. Under the Veterinary Medicines Regulations 2013, any VMD-registered online retailer can dispense prescription pet medicines (POM-V and POM-VPS) provided you supply a valid written prescription from your vet. General sale products (AVM-GSL) like basic flea collars and some wormers can be purchased without any prescription at all.

How do I get a pet prescription from my vet?

Ask your vet for a written prescription at the end of any consultation. Under the RCVS Code of Professional Conduct, vets must provide a written prescription if requested. Your vet may charge a small prescription fee (typically £10-£25), but the savings from buying online usually outweigh this. The prescription is valid for up to six months.

How long does delivery take from online pet pharmacies?

Most UK online pet pharmacies deliver within 2-5 working days using Royal Mail or courier services. Some offer next-day delivery for an additional fee. Delivery begins after the pharmacy has verified your prescription, which typically adds one working day.

Do I need a prescription for flea and worming treatments?

It depends on the product. Prescription-strength treatments like Bravecto, NexGard, and Advocate require a vet prescription (POM-V). However, many effective products are available as POM-VPS (any pharmacist or SQP can supply) or AVM-GSL (no prescription needed). See the OTC vs Prescription section above for a full breakdown.

How much cheaper is buying pet medicine online vs from my vet?

Online pharmacies typically sell pet medicines at 30-70% less than high-street vet prices. Even after the prescription fee (£10-£25), most owners save significantly on repeat medications. The savings are largest on long-term prescriptions for chronic conditions like arthritis, thyroid disorders, and heart disease.

Key Takeaway

Buying pet medicines online is legal, safe, and significantly cheaper than buying from your vet's dispensary. The process is simple: get a written prescription (£10-£25 fee), upload it to a VMD-registered pharmacy, and wait for delivery. For non-prescription products like Frontline, Drontal, and basic supplements, you do not even need a prescription. Always verify a pharmacy's VMD registration before purchasing, and never buy from a site that does not ask for your prescription. Your vet remains your first port of call for diagnosis and dosage advice, but where you buy the medicine is entirely your choice.
